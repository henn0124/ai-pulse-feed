Over the last 24–48 hours, Tier 1 AI/dev voices converged on one theme: **we’re moving from “AI writes snippets” to “AI runs workflows.”**

## 1) Karpathy: the unit of software is shifting from files to agents
Andrej Karpathy argued that the IDE isn’t dying—it’s expanding. In his framing, developers are moving up a level: programming increasingly means orchestrating agents, not just editing files. He also highlighted a practical risk: cloud/oauth outages now cause “intelligence brownouts,” where productivity drops sharply when AI systems fail.

**Why it matters:** reliability and observability of AI tooling are becoming core engineering concerns, not just “platform” concerns.

## 2) Cursor: new evaluation focus on agentic coding quality *and* efficiency
Cursor shared a method for scoring models on agentic coding tasks and emphasized combining offline benchmarks with online evals. They also shipped a wave of ecosystem plugins (GitLab, Atlassian, Datadog, Hugging Face, etc.), pointing to a more tool-connected coding loop.

**Why it matters:** model leaderboards alone are less useful; teams need evals tied to real task completion speed, cost, and correctness in production contexts.

## 3) Simon Willison: practical AI engineering over hype
Simon surfaced concrete, high-signal examples: autoresearch-assisted performance optimization work and guidance around AI improving code quality, not just output volume. He also amplified pricing predictability themes (e.g., safer experimentation in CI).

**Why it matters:** the center of gravity is shifting toward measurable engineering outcomes—faster optimization, safer CI automation, and less “demo theater.”

## 4) Milan Milanović: productivity surge, but debt risk is real
Milan’s posts reflected a dual narrative: software output is increasing, but many teams still default to coding too early and risk generating low-value features and tech debt with AI acceleration.

**Why it matters:** AI raises throughput, but product judgment and architecture discipline are now bigger bottlenecks.

## Key Takeaways
- AI coding is shifting from editing files to orchestrating agent workflows.
- Reliability of AI tooling is now a first-order productivity variable.
- Teams need workflow-level evals (quality, speed, cost), not just benchmark scores.
- Quality-adjusted velocity beats raw output volume.

The most important shift this week is not a new model release—it’s **operational maturity**:
- Treat AI as infrastructure (with failure modes)
- Evaluate agents on real workflows, not toy benchmarks
- Optimize for quality-adjusted velocity, not raw code volume

Teams that internalize these three will outperform teams that just “use more AI.”
